Funchal

Funchal is the enchanting capital of the island of Madeira. With its obvious charm and attraction the dynamic center of the Archipelago is nestled along the Atlantic and resembles a natural amphitheatre.

If you're visiting the beautiful city of Funchal, you should take a trip to the Monte Palace Tropical Gardens, situated in the village of Monte. The vast estate has a colorful history that dates back to the 18th century and was once used to host a hotel which can still be viewed and admired to this day.

The city derives its name from the plentiful sweet-smelling wild herb fennel and has long been a significant commercial centre in the Atlantic, with important local products like sugar and wine supporting its growing economy. This fascinating city, made up of ten churches, has a multitude of historical landmarks, museums, recreational places, beautiful landscaping, and stunning views.

Ponta do Sol

The lovely village of Ponta do Sol, which is known for having the most sunshine hours per year, is the perfect place to soak up the voluminous sunshine of Madeira's southwest coast. Beach lovers and outdoor enthusiasts both adore this wonderful location. Along with attracting tourists, Ponta do Sol is a significant agricultural hub that produces well-known products like bananas and sugar cane as well as supporting thriving horticulture and floriculture sectors. Discover Ponta do Sol with its breathtaking mountain views, charming villages, and inviting beaches.

Serrado

Eira do Serrado is a small village located in the heart of the island of Madeira, Portugal. It sits at an elevation of about 1,000 meters (3,280 feet) above sea level, nestled between two towering peaks - Pico do Arieiro and Pico das Torres. The village is known for its stunning panoramic views of Madeira's mountainous terrain and for its close proximity to the breathtaking Curral das Freiras (Valley of the Nuns).

The village is known for its natural beauty and is a popular destination for nature lovers and hikers The surrounding area is covered with lush greenery, and there are several trails and paths that lead to different viewpoints, including Pico do Arieiro, Madeira's third-highest peak.

One of the main attractions in Eira do Serrado is the Miradouro Eira do Serrado, a lookout point that offers sweeping views of the valley below. Visitors can also hike to the nearby Pico do Arieiro, which is the third-highest peak on the island and provides even more stunning views of the surrounding landscape.

Porto Moniz

Located in the north of the Island of Madeira, Porto Moniz is a municipality that has some of the most beautiful sceneries in the Region. Between the imposing mountains and the calm surface of the ocean, Porto Moniz also dazzles with the charm of its picturesque parishes, which overflow with rich customs.

This whole region is deeply marked by the botanical diversity of the Laurissilva Forest, which from its height confronts the imposing northern sea, through its ridges, cliffs and coves.

You can't talk about Porto Moniz without mentioning the natural pools in its volcanic rocks where their clear waters are renewed cyclically. The eternal ex-libris of this municipality.

Madeiran culture is as rich and diverse as its breathtaking landscapes. The island is a Portuguese archipelago known for its lush green landscapes, crystal-clear waters, and year-round mild weather. The island's culture has a rich and unique blend of influences by Portuguese, African, and British traditions, making it a vibrant and exciting place to visit.

One of the most distinctive aspects of Madeiran culture is its music. The island is home to the traditional folk music genre known as "Fado," which combines Portuguese and African rhythms to create a unique sound. Madeira also has its own distinct style of dance, called "Bailinho," which is characterized by its fast-paced steps and lively music.

Another significant part of Madeiran culture is its wine. In the early days of Madeira, the wine produced on the island was not very good. The journey from Madeira to the European mainland was long and arduous, and the wine would often spoil during transport.

However, in the 18th century, a solution was found. Shippers began adding brandy to the wine to preserve it during the long journey. They also discovered that the heat of the journey improved the flavor of the wine, and thus the tradition of heating Madeira wine was born. Today, Madeira wine is still heated during the aging process, which gives it a unique character and flavor.

During the 18th and 19th centuries, Madeira wine became very popular in Europe and America. It was a favorite drink of many historical figures, including George Washington, Thomas Jefferson, and William Shakespeare. In fact, Madeira was used to toast the signing of the Declaration of Independence in 1776.

Madeira's history is another important part of its culture. The island was discovered by Portuguese explorers in the 15th century and was a crucial stop for ships traveling between Europe and the Americas.

Madeira's strategic location led to the island being heavily fortified, and visitors can still see the remains of the many forts and watchtowers that were built to protect the island.

Jack's Travel Story

Jack, a 45-year-old entrepreneur from the United States.


Magazine Reporter: Jack, what brought you to Madeira Island?

Jack: I came to Madeira for a much-needed break from my busy life. I had read about the island's relaxing atmosphere, and it seemed like the perfect place to unwind and recharge.

Magazine Reporter: How did you experience the island during your stay?

Jack: Madeira was everything I hoped for and more. The island has a unique blend of old-world charm and modern amenities that made my stay comfortable and enjoyable. The locals were incredibly friendly and always willing to share their knowledge about the island's history and culture.

Magazine Reporter: What was your favorite part of visiting Madeira?

Jack: I really loved exploring the local markets and trying the regional food and wine. The passion fruit-flavored Poncha, a traditional Madeiran drink, became my favorite! The whole experience was a refreshing change from my daily routine, and I left Madeira feeling rejuvenated and inspired.

Ana's Travel Story

Ana, a 32 years old freelance photographer From Portugal.

Magazine Reporter: Ana, can you tell us what brought you to Madeira Island?

Ana: I've always wanted to visit Madeira since I heard about its stunning landscapes and diverse flora and fauna. As a photographer, I thought it would be the perfect place to explore and capture some breathtaking shots.

Magazine Reporter: How was your experience on the island?

Ana: It was simply magical. I loved the warm and welcoming people, the delicious local cuisine, and the vibrant cultural events. The island's natural beauty surpassed all my expectations from the dramatic cliffs and lush forests to the crystal-clear waters and charming villages. I especially enjoyed exploring the levadas (irrigation channels) and discovering hidden waterfalls.

Magazine Reporter: What did you like the most about Madeira?

Ana: For me, the highlight was hiking in the Laurisilva forest. The ancient trees, moss-covered rocks, and the sound of water flowing through the levadas created an enchanting atmosphere. I felt like I was in a fairy tale.
